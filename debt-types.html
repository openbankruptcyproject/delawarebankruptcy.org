Types of Debt in Delaware Bankruptcy

Which debts can be discharged in Delaware bankruptcy? Secured vs unsecured, priority vs non-priority, and non-dischargeable debts explained.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Debt Categories in Bankruptcy

Secured Debt

Backed by collateral. The creditor has a lien on specific property.

  • Mortgage (secured by your home)
  • Car loan (secured by vehicle)
  • Home equity loan / HELOC
  • Secured personal loans

In bankruptcy: The lien survives even if personal liability is discharged. You must continue paying to keep the property.

Unsecured Debt

No collateral. The creditor has no claim on specific property.

  • Credit cards
  • Medical bills
  • Personal loans
  • Utility bills
  • Collection accounts

In bankruptcy: Typically discharged in full (Chapter 7) or partially paid through your plan (Chapter 13).

Priority Debts

Priority debts receive special treatment in bankruptcy and must be paid before general unsecured creditors. In Chapter 13, priority debts must be paid in full through your repayment plan.

  • Domestic support obligations -- Child support and alimony (highest priority)
  • Administrative expenses -- Trustee fees, attorney fees for the case
  • Recent tax debts -- Income taxes within 3 years, trust fund taxes
  • Employee wages -- Up to $15,150 per employee earned within 180 days before filing

Non-Dischargeable Debts

Certain debts survive bankruptcy and cannot be eliminated regardless of which chapter you file. These are listed in 11 U.S.C. sections 523(a) and 1328(a):

  • Student loans -- Unless you prove undue hardship in an adversary proceeding
  • Child support and alimony -- Domestic support obligations are never dischargeable
  • Recent taxes -- Tax debts that do not meet the 3-year/2-year/240-day rules
  • Fraud -- Debts obtained through fraud, false pretenses, or false financial statements
  • DUI debts -- Debts arising from death or personal injury caused by driving under the influence
  • Criminal fines and restitution -- Court-ordered penalties from criminal cases
  • Willful and malicious injury -- Debts from intentional harm to another person or property

Frequently Asked Questions

What debts can be discharged in bankruptcy?

Most unsecured debts can be discharged including credit cards, medical bills, personal loans, utility bills, and old lease obligations. Secured debts (mortgage, car loan) can be discharged but the lien survives. Some debts like student loans require proving undue hardship.

What debts cannot be discharged?

Non-dischargeable debts include: most student loans (without proving undue hardship), child support and alimony, recent tax debts (within 3 years), debts from fraud or misrepresentation, DUI-related debts, criminal fines and restitution, and debts from willful and malicious injury.

What is the difference between secured and unsecured debt?

Secured debt is backed by collateral (mortgage secured by your home, car loan secured by your car). Unsecured debt has no collateral (credit cards, medical bills). In bankruptcy, secured creditors have rights to the collateral; unsecured creditors typically receive little or nothing.

What are priority debts in bankruptcy?

Priority debts must be paid in full before other unsecured debts receive anything. They include: domestic support obligations (child support, alimony), recent tax debts, wages owed to employees (up to a limit), and certain other claims specified in 11 U.S.C. section 507.

Can I discharge credit card debt in Delaware?

Yes. Credit card debt is general unsecured debt and is fully dischargeable in both Chapter 7 and Chapter 13. However, charges made within 90 days of filing that exceed $800 for luxury goods, or cash advances over $1,100 within 70 days, may be presumed non-dischargeable.

What happens to cosigned debts in bankruptcy?

In Chapter 7, your discharge eliminates your obligation but the cosigner remains fully liable. In Chapter 13, the codebtor stay (section 1301) temporarily protects cosigners from collection while your plan is active. If you pay the cosigned debt in full through your plan, the cosigner is protected permanently.
