Keeping Your House in Delaware Bankruptcy

How to keep your home when filing bankruptcy in Delaware. Homestead exemption ($125,000), Chapter 13 mortgage catch-up, and foreclosure defense.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Delaware Homestead Exemption

Exemption amount: $125,000

How equity works: If your home is worth $300,000 and you owe $250,000, your equity is $50,000. If that is within Delaware's homestead exemption, your home is fully protected in Chapter 7.

Delaware allows you to choose between state and federal exemptions.

Chapter 7 -- Keeping Your Home

In Chapter 7, you can keep your home if your equity is within the homestead exemption. You must continue making mortgage payments -- Chapter 7 does not eliminate mortgage liens. The trustee examines whether there is non-exempt equity that could be used to pay creditors.

  • Equity within exemption: You keep the home and continue payments
  • Equity exceeds exemption: The trustee may sell the home (rare if equity is modest)
  • Underwater home: No equity risk -- you keep the home as long as you pay

Chapter 13 -- Mortgage Catch-Up

Stopping Foreclosure

Filing Chapter 13 immediately triggers the automatic stay, which halts all foreclosure proceedings. Delaware uses judicial foreclosure (typically approximately 6 months or longer), so timing your filing is critical.

Curing Mortgage Arrears

Chapter 13 lets you catch up on missed mortgage payments through your 3-5 year repayment plan while making current payments going forward. This is one of the most powerful tools for saving your home.

Lien Stripping

If your home's value is less than what you owe on the first mortgage, a second mortgage or HELOC can be stripped (reclassified as unsecured debt) through your Chapter 13 plan. The second lienholder receives only a fraction of what is owed, and the lien is removed upon plan completion.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep my house if I file bankruptcy in Delaware?

Yes, in most cases. Delaware's homestead exemption protects up to $125,000 in home equity. If your equity is within the exemption, your home is protected in Chapter 7. Chapter 13 lets you catch up on missed mortgage payments over 3-5 years.

What is Delaware's homestead exemption?

Delaware's homestead exemption protects up to $125,000 in equity in your primary residence. Delaware allows you to choose between state and federal exemptions. Equity is your home's current value minus what you owe on it.

Can Chapter 13 stop foreclosure?

Yes. Filing Chapter 13 immediately stops foreclosure through the automatic stay. You then catch up on missed payments through your 3-5 year repayment plan while making current mortgage payments going forward. Delaware uses judicial foreclosure, which typically takes approximately 6 months or longer.

What happens to my mortgage in Chapter 7?

Chapter 7 does not eliminate your mortgage lien. If you want to keep the home, you continue making payments. If your equity exceeds the homestead exemption, the trustee may sell the home. Chapter 7 does eliminate personal liability for the mortgage debt if you choose to surrender.

Can I strip a second mortgage in Chapter 13?

If your home is worth less than what you owe on the first mortgage, a second mortgage or HELOC may be stripped (reclassified as unsecured debt) in Chapter 13. This is called lien stripping and is available in the District of Delaware.

How does judicial foreclosure work in Delaware?

Delaware uses judicial foreclosure, which typically takes approximately 6 months or longer. This is the timeline you are working against if you need to file bankruptcy to stop a foreclosure sale.
