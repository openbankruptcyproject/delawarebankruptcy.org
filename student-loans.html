Student Loans and Bankruptcy in Delaware

Can you discharge student loans in bankruptcy in Delaware? The Brunner test, undue hardship, income-driven repayment, and recent developments.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

The Undue Hardship Standard

Student loans -- both federal and private -- can be discharged in bankruptcy if you prove undue hardship through an adversary proceeding (a separate lawsuit within your bankruptcy case). In the District of Delaware, the court applies the Brunner test.

The Three Brunner Prongs

  1. Minimal standard of living: You cannot maintain a minimal standard of living for yourself and your dependents if forced to repay
  2. Persistence: Your financial hardship is likely to continue for a significant portion of the repayment period
  3. Good faith: You have made good-faith efforts to repay the loans

Recent Developments

DOJ Guidance (November 2022)

The Department of Justice issued new guidance creating a more standardized process for evaluating student loan discharge. Debtors can now submit an attestation form, and the DOJ has instructed its attorneys to consent to discharge when the evidence supports it. This represents a significant shift from the historical near-impossibility of student loan discharge.

Income-Driven Repayment as an Alternative

If discharge through bankruptcy is not viable, federal income-driven repayment (IDR) plans can reduce monthly payments to as low as $0 based on your income. After 20-25 years of payments, any remaining balance is forgiven. The SAVE plan (effective 2024) offers the most generous terms.

Student Loans by Chapter

Chapter 7

  • Must file separate adversary proceeding
  • Prove undue hardship (Brunner test)
  • If successful, loans are discharged
  • If unsuccessful, loans survive but other debts are eliminated
  • No repayment plan -- faster resolution

Chapter 13

  • Can file adversary proceeding for discharge
  • Student loans can be included in repayment plan
  • May pay reduced amount during plan
  • Automatic stay stops collection during plan
  • Unpaid balance survives unless hardship proven

Frequently Asked Questions

Can student loans be discharged in bankruptcy in Delaware?

Student loans can be discharged in bankruptcy in Delaware, but only if you prove undue hardship to the court through an adversary proceeding. The standard used in the District of Delaware is the Brunner test, which requires showing you cannot maintain a minimal standard of living, your situation is likely to persist, and you have made good-faith efforts to repay.

What is the Brunner test?

The Brunner test requires proving three things: (1) you cannot maintain a minimal standard of living for yourself and your dependents if forced to repay, (2) your financial situation is likely to persist for a significant portion of the repayment period, and (3) you have made good-faith efforts to repay the loans.

What about the new DOJ guidance on student loans?

In November 2022, the DOJ issued new guidance making it easier to discharge student loans in bankruptcy. The DOJ now uses a standardized process to evaluate undue hardship claims, and debtors can submit an attestation form. This has made discharges more accessible than before.

Should I include student loans in my bankruptcy filing?

Yes. Even if you cannot discharge them, listing student loans in your bankruptcy stops collection activity during the case through the automatic stay. In Chapter 13, student loan payments can be structured into your repayment plan, sometimes at reduced amounts.

What happens to student loans in Chapter 13?

In Chapter 13, student loans are treated as general unsecured claims unless you file a separate adversary proceeding for discharge. You can propose paying a reduced amount through your plan, but any unpaid balance survives the bankruptcy unless you prove undue hardship.

Are private student loans treated differently?

Private student loans and federal student loans are both subject to the same undue hardship standard for discharge. However, some courts have been more willing to discharge private student loans, particularly if the school was not an eligible institution.
