Keeping Your Car in Delaware Bankruptcy

How to keep your car when filing bankruptcy in Delaware. Vehicle exemption ($15,000), reaffirmation, redemption, and Chapter 13 cramdown options.

This page provides general educational information, not legal advice. Consult a qualified attorney for advice about your specific situation.

Delaware Vehicle Exemption

Exemption amount: $15,000 per debtor

How equity works: If your car is worth $12,000 and you owe $10,000, your equity is $2,000. If that is within Delaware's vehicle exemption, the car is protected.

Delaware allows you to choose between state and federal exemptions, so compare both to see which protects more of your vehicle equity.

Options in Chapter 7

Reaffirmation

Sign a reaffirmation agreement to keep paying your car loan. You keep the car and the debt survives the bankruptcy. Monthly payments continue as before. Your attorney must certify it does not impose undue hardship.

Redemption

Pay the current fair market value of the car in a lump sum, regardless of how much you owe. If you owe $18,000 on a car worth $10,000, you pay $10,000 and keep the car. Some lenders specialize in redemption financing.

Surrender

If you no longer want or need the car, you can surrender it. The remaining loan balance is discharged along with your other debts. This may make sense if the car is worth much less than what you owe.

Options in Chapter 13

Keep the Car and Catch Up

Chapter 13 lets you keep your car while catching up on missed payments through your 3-5 year repayment plan. The automatic stay stops repossession the moment you file.

Cramdown (910-Day Rule)

If you purchased your car more than 910 days (about 2.5 years) before filing, you can reduce the loan balance to the car's current market value under 11 U.S.C. section 1325(a). Example: if you owe $20,000 on a car worth $12,000, you pay $12,000 through your plan at a court-approved interest rate. The remaining $8,000 is treated as unsecured debt.

The 910-day rule: If you purchased the car within 910 days of filing, you must pay the full loan balance -- no cramdown is available.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can I keep my car if I file bankruptcy in Delaware?

Yes, in most cases. Delaware's vehicle exemption protects up to $15,000 in equity per vehicle. Delaware allows you to choose between state and federal exemptions, so compare both to see which protects more of your vehicle equity. If your equity is within the exemption, you can keep your car in Chapter 7. Chapter 13 lets you keep your car while catching up on payments.

What is the vehicle exemption in Delaware?

Delaware's vehicle exemption is $15,000 per debtor. Equity is the difference between your car's current value and what you owe on it. If your equity is below the exemption amount, the car is fully protected.

What is reaffirmation in bankruptcy?

Reaffirmation is an agreement to keep paying your car loan as if the bankruptcy never happened. You keep the car but remain personally liable for the debt. Your attorney must certify it does not impose undue hardship, and the court must approve it.

What is redemption in Chapter 7?

Redemption lets you pay the current market value of the vehicle in a single lump sum, regardless of how much you owe. If you owe $15,000 on a car worth $8,000, you can keep the car by paying $8,000. The challenge is coming up with the lump sum.

What is a Chapter 13 cramdown for a car?

If you purchased your car more than 910 days before filing Chapter 13, you can reduce the loan balance to the car's current market value. You pay this reduced amount through your 3-5 year plan at a court-approved interest rate. This is called a cramdown under 11 U.S.C. section 1325(a).

What happens to my car loan in bankruptcy in Delaware?

It depends on the chapter and your choices. In Chapter 7, you can reaffirm (keep paying), redeem (pay current value), or surrender the car. In Chapter 13, you can keep the car and catch up on missed payments or cramdown the loan balance.
